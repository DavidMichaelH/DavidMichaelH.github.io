Formula for Total Variation Distance

Theorem

Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be probability measures on \(\mathcal{X}\) that are absolutely continuous with respect to the measure \[ \mu = \frac{P+Q}{2}. \] Let the total variation distance between \(P\) and \(Q\) be denoted by \[ TV(P,Q)= \sup_{A \in \mathcal{F}} |P(A) - Q(A)|. \] Then, \[ TV(P,Q) = \frac{1}{2} \int |p(x) - q(x)|\, d\mu, \] where \(p = \frac{dP}{d\mu}\) and \(q = \frac{dQ}{d\mu}\).

Proof

First, let \(A\) be a set that achieves the supremum in the definition of the total variation distance. Then, without loss of generality, \[ TV(P,Q) = P(A) - Q(A). \] Then indeed, \[ TV(P,Q) = \int_A dP - \int_A dQ = \int_A (p - q)\, d\mu. \] We can write this as \[ = \int_{A \cap \{p>q\}} |p-q|\, d\mu - \int_{A \cap \{p < q\}} |p-q|\, d\mu. \] This expression is maximized when we take \[ A = \{ x : p(x) > q(x) \}. \] Similarly,

\[ TV(P,Q) = P(A)-Q(A) = Q(A^c)-P(A^c) = \int_{\{q > p\}} |p-q|\, d\mu. \]
\[ TV(P,Q) = P(A)-Q(A) = Q(A^c)-P(A^c) \] \[ = \int_{\{q > p\}} |p-q|\, d\mu. \]
Thus, we have \[ TV(P,Q) = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\{p>q\}} |p-q|\, d\mu + \frac{1}{2}\int_{\{q>p\}} |p-q|\, d\mu, \] \[ = \frac{1}{2} \int |p-q|\, d\mu. \]
